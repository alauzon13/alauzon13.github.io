Wampum Belts: Data Preservation through Oral Histories

By Adele Lauzon

After centuries of exploitation, misrepresentation, and exclusion from stewardship, Indigenous Peoples from across North America are working to reclaim their data.

Data has long been weaponized against Indigenous peoples. Early census data was used to justify forceful assimilation policies, like the Indian Act in Canada; the lack of race- or ethnicity-disaggregated law enforcement data has allowed for the crisis of Missing and Murdered Indigenous Women to become invisible; regular destruction of residential school recordshas obscured information about how many children died in this system.

Just to name a few.

Now, it's time for Indigenous Peoples to be in charge of their own data.

Indigenous Data Sovereignty

The path to Indigenous Data Sovereignty involves First Nations, Inuit, and Métis peoples owning and controlling data that is created with or about them. It's about Indigenous Peoples moving from subjects to researchers. It's about accepting Indigenous data, in its various forms, as equally valid as Western knowledge.

Prioritizing Indigenous Data Sovereignty is crucial when Western and Indigenous knowledge systems have opposing records of the same events, as is often the case when it comes to treaties.

While many treaty disputes have centered on Indigenous records stored through oral tradition (the Syliboy case, 1928; the Marshall Case, 1993), the Haudenosaunee Wampum Belts are a unique example of how oral history and Indigenous artistry intersect as a means of recordkeeping. Despite Western rejection of the Wampum Belts as a valid record of a treaty made between the Haudenosaunee and the Dutch in 1613, the dynamic, relational, resilient nature of this data system is an important example of Indigenous Data Sovereignty in practice.

Indigenous and Western Knowledge Systems

In the Western worldview, knowledge is gained through observation and experimentation. Western knowledge is produced in specific, controlled environments, yet also hinges upon generalizability to other contexts. Western ways of knowing also reinforce the idea that knowledge obtained outside this experimental framework is less valid.

On the other hand, Indigenous Knowledge is informed by land and the environment. Indigenous approaches to research are varied, but often include a relational element -- accounting for experiences and spirituality. Indigenous data exists in diverse forms, including regalia, songs, art, ceremonies, and oral traditions. A critical part of Indigenous Data Sovereignty is recognizing and respecting data that exists outside of Western definitions and parameters.

While frameworks like Two Eyed Seeing work to hold these worldviews in tandem, there are still instances where Indigenous and Western knowledge systems maintain different records of the same events.

The Two-Row Wampum

Wampum belts are intricate beaded artworks made out of shells, and are thought of as a living record of the Haudenosaunee nation (present-day New York State and Ontario). Wampum can symbolize many types of events — including the granting of titles, technological innovations, treaties, and more. The belts are often used as a guide to share oral traditions or as part of public demonstrations.

One of the most famous Wampum Belts is the Two Row Wampum, or Kaswentha (Gaswéñdah), representing a treaty of "respectful coexistence" between the Haudenosaunee and the Dutch in 1613. When Dutch settlers first arrived on Haudenosaunee land and suggested that Indigenous Peoples refer to them as “father,” the Haudenosaunee proposed “brother” instead. This autonomy and sovereignty is reflected in the design of the belt, made from purple quahog shells and white whelk snail shells, displaying two horizontal lines.

One purple line represents the Haudeonsaunee canoe, and the other the Dutch tall ship. Between the purple lines is a row of white shells representing the River of Life, upon which the canoe and tall ship are both travelling, neither steering nor crossing the other.

At the time of the treaty’s inception, the Haudenosaunee did not use paper to record their history. Because there is no original paper-based documentation of the treaty, some Western scholars question its legitimacy. Wampum Belts and their accompanying oral histories exist outside the Western constraints of what data can be, and as a result are often dismissed entirely.

The Resilience of Alternative Data Storage

Wampum Belts form a resilient data system unlike any Western method. After Chief Deskaheh toured Europe to garner global support for Haudenosaunee sovereignty in 1923, the Canadian government ordered the RCMP to dismantle the Confederacy and destroy the Wampum Belts.
However, Wampum Belts and their accompanying oral histories are a living, dynamic database -- meaning that destruction did not result in missing data. With the guidance of Indigenous Knowledge Holders, the belts were remade and the database persists. Wampum Belts do not store data in a server or a spreadsheet, but instead store information in the community.
The relational nature of the Wampum system epitomises the Indigenous worldview of knowledge creation and sharing. Given how destructive colonial forces were and are, we must consider that the Indigenous worldview and data methods like the Wampum Belt may be more resilient than the Western counterparts.
Scientific research has expanded to accept not just spreadsheets and tables as data, but images, videos, novels, audio, and much more as valid sources of information. The Wampum system is an invitation to expand our understanding of data even further, and to challenge Western beliefs around what "good" data looks like.
